About Medical Weight Loss
Understanding how medication supports your journey.
❤️‍🔥 What is Medical Weight Loss?
⌄
Medical weight loss uses clinically approved medications alongside lifestyle changes to support significant, sustained weight loss. Unlike dieting alone, these treatments work with your body's own hormones to reduce appetite, improve blood sugar regulation, and help you feel fuller for longer.

The NHS and NICE (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) have approved several medications for weight management in adults who meet specific criteria — typically a BMI of 30 or above, or 27 with a weight-related health condition.

Medication is a tool, not a shortcut. The NHS is clear that it works best when combined with changes to diet, physical activity, and lifestyle.
🔬 How do the medications work?
⌄
Different medications work in different ways. Currently used medications in the UK include:

  • GLP-1 receptor agonists (e.g. semaglutide / Wegovy, liraglutide / Saxenda) — mimic a hormone that regulates appetite and blood sugar. You feel fuller faster and eat less.
  • GIP and GLP-1 agonists (e.g. tirzepatide / Mounjaro) — work on two hormone pathways. NICE approved for weight management in 2023.
  • Orlistat (Xenical / Alli) — reduces how much fat your body absorbs from food.
  • Mysimba (bupropion / naltrexone) — affects the brain's hunger and reward system.
Your prescriber will have chosen the right medication based on your health profile. Always follow their specific guidance.
🏃 Why lifestyle still matters
⌄
The NHS is clear that medication works best as part of a broader programme that includes changes to what you eat and how active you are. Research shows that people who combine medication with regular exercise and improved nutrition lose significantly more weight — and keep it off for longer — compared to medication alone.

This is where Impact Coaching comes in. Working with Ritchie alongside your medication is not an optional extra — it is a central part of getting real, lasting results. Exercise during medical weight loss helps preserve muscle mass, improves energy levels, supports mental wellbeing, and protects your long-term health.

Exercise
Exercise on Medication
Why training matters more than ever on this journey.
💪 Why strength training is essential
⌄
When you lose weight — whether through diet, medication, or both — your body loses a mix of fat and muscle. Losing muscle is not just a cosmetic concern. Muscle drives your metabolism, keeps you strong, and protects your long-term health.

Strength training is the single most effective way to preserve muscle while losing fat. Research consistently shows it reduces the proportion of weight lost from muscle during a calorie deficit. On weight loss medication, where food intake can be significantly reduced, this is even more important.

Aim for at least 2 strength sessions per week alongside any cardio activity.
📋 NHS activity guidelines
⌄
The NHS recommends adults aim for:

  • 150 minutes of moderate activity per week (e.g. brisk walking, cycling, swimming) — or 75 minutes of vigorous activity (e.g. running, aerobics)
  • Strength exercises on 2 or more days per week that work all major muscle groups
  • Break up long periods of sitting throughout the day
If you're new to exercise or returning after a break, start with shorter sessions and build up gradually. Something is always better than nothing.
⚡ Managing energy and fatigue
⌄
Fatigue and low energy are common in the early weeks on weight loss medication, particularly as your body adjusts to eating less. This is completely normal.

Tips for training when energy is low:
  • Time your sessions for when you typically feel best (morning, afternoon, or evening)
  • Eat a small, protein-containing snack 1–2 hours before training if you can manage it
  • Reduce intensity temporarily — lighter weights and shorter sessions still count
  • Stay hydrated — dehydration makes fatigue much worse
  • Be honest with Ritchie about how you're feeling — sessions are always adapted to suit where you're at
Energy levels typically improve after the first few weeks as your body settles into the medication.
🚶 Starting out — build gradually
⌄
If you haven't been active regularly, the NHS recommends starting small and building up over weeks. Even 10-minute walks make a real difference to start with.

A simple starting approach:
  • Week 1–2: 2 x 20-minute walks + 1 light strength session
  • Week 3–4: 3 x 30-minute walks + 2 light strength sessions
  • Week 5+: build toward NHS recommendations at your own pace
The most important thing is consistency over intensity. Short workouts done regularly build the habit that creates long-term results.

Nutrition
Nutrition Basics
When you eat less, what you eat matters more.
🥩 Protein first — why it matters so much
⌄
On weight loss medication your appetite is reduced, which means your total food intake drops significantly. This makes every meal count — and protein is the most important priority.

Protein helps you:
  • Preserve muscle mass while losing fat
  • Feel fuller for longer after meals
  • Support recovery from exercise
  • Maintain energy levels
Good protein sources: chicken, turkey, fish, eggs, Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, lentils, beans, tofu, low-fat dairy. Try to include a protein source at every meal, even if the portion is small.
🍽️ NHS Eatwell Guide basics
⌄
The NHS Eatwell Guide shows the balance of food groups for a healthy diet. Even on medication, these proportions remain a useful guide:

  • Fruit and vegetables — aim for 5 portions a day. Variety matters.
  • Starchy carbohydrates — choose higher fibre versions (wholegrain bread, brown rice, oats) in smaller portions than you previously ate
  • Protein — prioritise lean sources (see above)
  • Dairy or alternatives — lower-fat, lower-sugar versions where possible
  • Oils and spreads — small amounts of unsaturated fats (olive oil, rapeseed oil)
Foods high in fat, salt and sugar should be eaten less often and in smaller amounts.
💧 Hydration — more important than usual
⌄
Weight loss medications can increase the risk of dehydration, particularly if you're experiencing nausea, vomiting, or diarrhoea. The NHS recommends aiming for 6–8 cups (about 1.5–2 litres) of fluid per day.

Hydration tips:
  • Water and low-sugar drinks are best
  • Carry a water bottle and sip regularly through the day
  • If nausea makes drinking hard, try small, frequent sips of cold water
  • Herbal teas and diluted squash count toward your fluid intake
  • Avoid excessive caffeine and alcohol — both contribute to dehydration
🍱 Eating with a reduced appetite
⌄
One of the effects of medication is eating much less than before. The risk is that small meals become nutrient-poor. Small adjustments make a big difference:

  • Eat slowly — fullness signals take time to reach the brain. Rushing often leads to discomfort
  • Stop when you feel satisfied, not full — overeating on medication can cause significant nausea
  • Avoid high-fat, high-sugar foods when appetite is low — they fill your calorie allowance with little nutritional value
  • Smaller, more frequent meals may suit you better than three large ones
  • If appetite is very low, protein shakes or smoothies can help you meet nutritional needs
If you are struggling to eat enough to function, speak to your prescriber.
🚫 What to limit or avoid
⌄
On weight loss medication, some foods are particularly likely to cause problems or undermine your results:

  • High-fat foods — fatty meats, fried food, full-fat pastries. These slow digestion and can worsen nausea on GLP-1 medications
  • Very sugary foods and drinks — cause blood sugar spikes and are calorie-dense with little nutritional value
  • Alcohol — the NHS advises keeping alcohol to a minimum. It can interact with some medications and is high in empty calories
  • Ultra-processed snacks — very easy to overeat and offer little nutritional benefit
This isn't about perfection. Small, consistent improvements are what create lasting change.

Side Effects
Managing Side Effects
What's common, what helps, and when to seek advice.
⚠️ Medical note
This section provides general information based on NHS guidance. Always discuss your side effects with your prescriber or pharmacist — they can advise whether to adjust your dose, timing, or medication.
🤢 Nausea — the most common side effect
⌄
Nausea is the most commonly reported side effect, particularly in the early weeks and after dose increases. For most people it improves significantly within 4–8 weeks as the body adjusts.

Practical tips to reduce nausea:
  • Eat smaller meals — overfilling the stomach is a major trigger
  • Eat slowly and chew thoroughly
  • Avoid lying down immediately after eating
  • Stay upright for at least 30 minutes after meals
  • Avoid strong food smells if they trigger nausea
  • Cold or room-temperature foods are often better tolerated than hot meals
  • Ginger tea or ginger biscuits can help mild nausea (NHS supported)
  • Keep sipping water, even in small amounts
If nausea is severe or preventing you from eating or drinking, contact your prescriber.
🚽 Digestive changes
⌄
Constipation and diarrhoea are both reported with weight loss medications, though constipation is more common on GLP-1 type treatments as they slow digestion.

For constipation:
  • Increase fluid intake — this is the most important step
  • Add more fibre gradually (vegetables, fruits, wholegrains, legumes)
  • Stay active — movement helps bowel function
  • Speak to your pharmacist about whether a gentle laxative is appropriate
For diarrhoea:
  • Stay well hydrated — replace fluids lost
  • Eat plain, easy-to-digest foods (e.g. toast, plain rice, bananas)
  • Avoid fatty, spicy, or very rich foods until settled
  • Contact your prescriber if it persists beyond a few days
😴 Fatigue and low energy
⌄
Feeling tired, especially in the first few weeks, is common. This can be caused by the medication itself, by eating significantly less, or by the adjustment period as your body changes.

Helping with fatigue:
  • Prioritise protein and nutritious food — fatigue is much worse when nutrition is poor
  • Maintain a consistent sleep schedule (see Lifestyle section)
  • Light exercise actually helps — even a 15-minute walk can improve energy
  • Avoid over-restricting calories — extremely low intake accelerates fatigue
  • Stay hydrated — even mild dehydration significantly increases tiredness
If fatigue is severe or doesn't improve after the first month, discuss it with your prescriber.
🚨 When to seek medical advice
⌄
Most side effects are mild and manageable. However, contact your prescriber or GP — or call 111 — if you experience:

  • Severe or persistent vomiting preventing you from keeping food or water down
  • Severe abdominal pain that radiates to your back — this can be a sign of pancreatitis and needs urgent assessment
  • Signs of a serious allergic reaction — swelling of the face or throat, difficulty breathing, severe rash
  • Changes in mood or mental health — depression, suicidal thoughts (rare but important to report)
  • Heart palpitations or chest pain
  • Vision changes (particularly relevant with some medications used in people with diabetes)
If in doubt, contact your prescriber or call 111.

Lifestyle
Lifestyle & Wellbeing
The habits that support everything else.
😴 Sleep and weight loss
⌄
Sleep has a direct effect on weight, hunger hormones, and the success of weight loss efforts. The NHS recommends most adults aim for 7–9 hours per night.

Poor sleep increases levels of the hunger hormone ghrelin and reduces leptin (the fullness hormone), making it harder to stick to reduced eating — even on medication.

NHS sleep hygiene tips:
  • Keep to a consistent sleep and wake time — even at weekends
  • Make your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet
  • Avoid screens for at least an hour before bed
  • Limit caffeine after 2pm
  • Avoid large meals close to bedtime — this is particularly important on medication as digestion is already slower
  • Regular physical activity improves sleep quality significantly
🧠 Stress and emotional eating
⌄
Stress triggers the release of cortisol, which can increase appetite and cravings — particularly for high-fat, high-sugar foods. Medication may reduce physical hunger but does not always address emotional eating patterns.

Managing stress:
  • Regular exercise is one of the most effective stress relievers (NHS recommended)
  • NHS talking therapies are available free via GP referral or self-referral at nhs.uk/mental-health/talking-therapies
  • Mindfulness and breathing exercises — NHS offers free resources at nhs.uk/mental-health/self-help
  • Keep a simple food and mood diary to spot patterns between stress and eating
If emotional eating is a significant challenge, it is worth discussing with your GP. Psychological support alongside medication produces better long-term outcomes.
💜 Mental health on this journey
⌄
The mental and emotional side of medical weight loss is often underestimated. Changes to your body, relationship with food, and daily habits can bring up a range of feelings — some positive, some difficult.

This is normal. Some things worth knowing:
  • It's common to feel emotionally complex about rapid changes in appetite and body image
  • Some people experience improved mood as they become more active and healthier — others find the adjustment harder
  • If you experience persistent low mood, anxiety, or any thoughts of self-harm, speak to your GP — do not wait
  • Peer support from others on similar journeys can be very valuable (see Resources)
Your mental health matters as much as your physical health on this journey.
📉 Thinking long-term
⌄
NICE guidance is clear that the lifestyle changes made alongside medication are what determine long-term outcomes. Medication is most effective when used as a bridge to lasting habit change — not as a permanent replacement for lifestyle.

Building habits that last:
  • Focus on one or two changes at a time rather than trying to overhaul everything at once
  • Regular exercise builds a foundation that supports health regardless of medication status
  • Improved sleep, reduced stress, and better nutrition create a compounding effect over time
  • Progress is not always linear — weeks that feel stalled are a normal part of the process
The goal is a version of yourself that feels well, strong, and in control — not just a lower number on the scales.

Workout Guide
Workout Guide
NHS and NICE recommended exercises — no gym required to start.
These exercises are drawn from NHS physical activity guidance and the NHS Strength and Flex programme. They are starting points — if you're working with Ritchie, he will tailor sets, reps and progression around your individual level, energy, and how you're responding to medication.
🚶 Cardio — get your heart going
⌄
The NHS recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate cardio activity per week. These are the most accessible options:

Brisk walking Cycling Swimming Water aerobics Low-impact aerobics Dancing
Moderate intensity means you can still hold a conversation but you feel your breathing and heart rate increase. A brisk 30-minute walk 5 days a week meets the full NHS weekly target.

Start with whatever you can manage — even 10-minute walks count and can be built upon.
💪 Lower body strength
⌄
The NHS Strength and Flex programme includes these lower body exercises as core recommendations. The legs are your largest muscle group — working them has the biggest impact on metabolism and muscle preservation.

Squats Lunges Step-ups Glute bridges Calf raises Leg press (gym) Romanian deadlift
All of these can be done with bodyweight to start. Resistance bands or light dumbbells can be added as you progress. Correct form is especially important to avoid injury — this is something Ritchie covers in every session.
🤸 Upper body strength
⌄
NHS Strength and Flex upper body exercises — these work the chest, back, shoulders and arms:

Press-ups (or wall press-ups) Seated row (band or cable) Shoulder press Lat pulldown (gym) Chest press (dumbbells) Bicep curls Tricep dips
Wall press-ups and chair-supported versions are perfect starting points if standard press-ups are too challenging. The NHS advises starting with what you can manage and progressing when it feels comfortable.
🏋️ Core and stability
⌄
A strong core supports every other movement and reduces injury risk. These exercises feature in NHS and NICE-recommended physical activity programmes:

Plank (or box plank) Dead bug Bird dog Seated core twists Hip circles Pelvic tilts
Start with shorter holds and fewer reps — quality of movement matters more than quantity. Box plank (on your knees) is a good starting point for the standard plank.
🧘 Flexibility and recovery
⌄
The NHS recommends including flexibility and balance work as part of a rounded exercise routine, particularly for adults. This is often overlooked but supports recovery, reduces muscle soreness, and improves joint health.

Standing quad stretch Hamstring stretch Hip flexor stretch Shoulder rolls Chest opener Neck stretches Yoga (beginner)
Hold each stretch for 20–30 seconds without bouncing. Stretching is most effective after a workout when muscles are warm. Yoga and Pilates are both NHS-recommended for flexibility and wellbeing.
📅 Putting it together — a simple week
⌄
Based on NHS guidance for adults on a weight management programme, a simple starting week could look like this:

  • Monday — Strength session (lower body focus)
  • Tuesday — 30-minute brisk walk
  • Wednesday — Rest or gentle stretching
  • Thursday — Strength session (upper body + core)
  • Friday — 30-minute brisk walk or light cardio
  • Saturday — Active hobby (swimming, cycling, gardening)
  • Sunday — Rest
This is a template, not a prescription. If you're training with Ritchie, your programme is built specifically around your fitness level, schedule, and how your body is responding to medication. Adapt as needed — any movement is always better than none.
